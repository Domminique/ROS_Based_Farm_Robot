                    • kushalvyas Mod Sybil C • 4 months ago

                      Hi, when you are using cylindrical projections, keep the following in mind.
                      1) You'll need to establish a fixed point of taking photographs. Mount your camera on a stand, and only rotate it about the axis, while capturing the images
                      2) compute your focal length and (x,y) camera centers using the camera calibration (you can use the matlab toolbox for your convenience)
                      3) Warp into cylindrical space. You can use MSR's Image Stitching and Alignment Tutorial for details
                      4) Now that you converted from Cartesian to cylindrical image spaces, all you need to do is establish a affine transform, or frankly, just a translational matrix , that will take care of the camera motion. And then you align your images as per the translation between the two consecutive frame that you captured. You can also use the PASSTA Dataset to test your stitching algorithm.

                              • kushalvyas Mod Hansal Shah • 4 months ago

                                Okay I'll explain in a bit why those calculations are necessary.

                                As you know, the homography equation is I1 = H x I2. That means that your are transforming I2, with respect to I1. Now, throughout the code I follow this convention. Take a pair of images (I1, I2). If you compute homography using the above relation, you'll end up with a tranformed I2. And since here
                                I1 = some image
                                I2 = some image right to I1 (cause if you read, our input sequences is images in a left - to - right order)

                                So essentially, if I was to warp the right image (I2) onto I1, then I can simply use homography. Check the rightshift function . You will see I have directly used H to warp I2 on I1.

                                But, If I want to center the image subset on I2, and warp I1 onto I2, then I am proceeding in a reverse direction. Yes ? Inverse of a homography matrix is a transform that will map I1 onto I2. Hence the inverse.

                                As for your second question,
                                When I take an inverse of the H matrix, and transform I1, essentially, I need to take care of any negative indices. If I2 is the origin (center of subsequence), I cannot have negative values in my image right ? So I am basically handling offset, to bring it to the correct coordinate space. Read about how a matrix is used for transformations (rotational, scaling and translation) .
